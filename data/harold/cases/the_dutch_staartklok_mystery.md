HAROLD JENNINGS — CASE STUDY
Subject: The Dutch Staartklok and the Invisible Crutch Pin
Written from bench notes, 1989, with additions 2005
414 Marietta Avenue, Lancaster, PA

---

I've referred to this case in conversations with apprentices more times than I can count. I'm writing it up in full because the lesson is important enough to deserve more than a passing mention.

---

THE CLOCK

A Dutch Staartklok — literally "tail clock" in Dutch, also called a Frisian clock. The name comes from the pendulum, which is visible below the movement in an open arc rather than enclosed in a case. The style originated in Friesland (northern Netherlands) and was made in quantity from the late 17th century through the early 20th.

This particular clock was brought to me by Marcus van der Berg, a retired Dutch engineer who had emigrated to Lancaster County in the 1960s. The clock had belonged to his grandfather's family in Friesland. Movement was brass, typical of quality Dutch work of the period — probably made around 1820. The case was painted with traditional Frisian folk art: roosters, tulips, a farmstead scene. On the side panels, the traditional "Amsterdam ears" — decorative painted panels in the shape of stylized leaves or ears. Charming and specific.

The movement: eight-day, time and strike, with alarm. The alarm is integral to the movement rather than added on — a relatively early and well-made integration. The strike mechanism uses a count wheel (locking plate), not the rack and snail I usually see in American clocks. Count wheels require care during reassembly — the wheel's position at rest must correspond correctly to the last struck hour, or the strike sequence will be permanently offset.

---

THE PRESENTING PROBLEM

The clock stopped. Simple statement. Less simple reality.

Marcus had been dealing with this for almost two years before he came to me. He'd had the clock "serviced" twice by two other repairers — I won't name them — and both times it ran for a week or two and then stopped. He was, understandably, frustrated. He'd been told once that the problem was a worn bushing (fixed), and once that the mainspring needed replacement (replaced). After both services, the clock stopped again.

I want to be clear: worn bushings and mainspring problems can cause a clock to stop. In this case, I believe both diagnoses were partially correct in that these things were found and were worth addressing. But they were not the cause of the clock stopping.

---

THE INVESTIGATION

My examination began the way all my examinations begin: full disassembly, cleaning, inspection, documentation.

What I found during inspection:

- The mainspring was new — recently replaced, as reported. Correct size for the barrel.
- Bushings: the two that had been replaced were correctly fitted. I found no additional worn bushings requiring attention.
- The escape wheel: intact, no damaged teeth under magnification.
- The pallets (this is a recoil anchor escapement, appropriate for the period): worn slightly on the impulse faces, but within serviceable range. I polished them lightly.
- The count wheel strike mechanism: correctly positioned, no damage.
- The alarm mechanism: functional.

I reassembled and started the clock. It ran well. I let it run for two days under observation.

On the morning of the second day, I was at the bench working on another clock and I heard the tick slow. I looked up. The pendulum was still swinging, but the tick had become irregular — "tick... tock" instead of "tick tock." I watched. After about a minute, the escapement stopped completely. The pendulum continued for several more swings on its own momentum, then also stopped.

I had seen the failure occur. The pendulum was swinging but the escapement wasn't following it. This localized the fault to the connection between the pendulum and the escapement — specifically, the crutch.

---

THE CRUTCH

The crutch is the link between the pallet arbor and the pendulum. It's a forked piece of wire or rod, attached to the pallet arbor. The pendulum rod passes through the fork of the crutch, and a small pin (the crutch pin) engages a slot in the pendulum rod called the crutch slot.

As the pendulum swings, it pushes on the crutch pin, which drives the crutch, which drives the pallet arbor, which drives the pallets. Simultaneously, the escaping wheel teeth push on the pallets, which push on the crutch, which push on the pendulum, keeping it in motion. This is the connection between the timekeeping element and the escapement.

If this connection fails intermittently — if the crutch pin sometimes fails to engage the crutch slot — the pendulum and escapement become decoupled. The pendulum swings on its own until friction stops it. The escapement doesn't run without the pendulum's driving input.

I examined the crutch pin. At normal magnification (5x), it looked fine. I cleaned it, replaced it in the crutch, and tested again. Still failing. At 10x magnification, I found it.

The pin had a flat spot on one side. Not from normal wear — the flat spot was at an angle that didn't correspond to the direction of normal engagement wear. What I believe had happened is that at some earlier point in the clock's history, someone had replaced the crutch pin with a pin made from slightly softer material. Over decades of use, the repeated engagement with the crutch slot had produced normal wear — but because the material was soft, one area had deformed rather than worn evenly. The result was a flat spot that caused the pin to occasionally fail to engage the slot when the pendulum was at a specific arc position.

And here's the critical piece: the pendulum only passes through that specific arc position when the clock is cold. When the shop temperature dropped below about 65°F overnight, the pendulum rod's flexibility characteristic changed very slightly — just enough that at the critical arc position, the crutch pin would miss the slot. During the day, with the shop warmer, the pendulum's behavior at that arc was slightly different and the pin engaged normally.

This is why the clock had stopped twice after seemingly correct service. The previous repairers had fixed real problems — the bushing, the mainspring — but neither had examined the crutch pin carefully enough to find a fault that only manifested in specific temperature conditions.

---

THE RESOLUTION

I replaced the crutch pin with correct material — a piece of steel wire of appropriate diameter and hardness, correctly shaped and polished. The new pin was fitted snugly in the crutch and tested.

The clock ran for two weeks on my bench, through several cold nights. It did not stop.

Marcus collected it. I explained what I had found and why it had been difficult to diagnose. He listened carefully — he was an engineer, he appreciated precision in failure analysis. He thanked me and took the clock home.

It ran without stopping until his death in 2003. His son, whom I never met, sent me a note to say so. I've kept that note.

---

LESSONS

1. When a clock has been "serviced" before and is still failing, be suspicious of the previous diagnosis. Previous repairers found real problems; those problems were worth fixing. But find the problem that was missed, not just the problems that were found.

2. Intermittent failures require observation. You cannot diagnose a fault that only occurs under specific conditions by bench-testing at normal conditions. I ran this clock for two days under observation specifically because I'd been warned it was intermittent. Most intermittent faults will reveal themselves if you watch long enough.

3. Temperature is a variable. Materials change slightly with temperature. A fault that only occurs when cold is easy to miss if you only test a warm clock. If a customer reports problems that seem to occur seasonally or at certain times of day, temperature should be on your list of variables.

4. Examine the crutch and crutch pin carefully. This component is often overlooked because it's simple-looking. It is mechanically simple. But its correct function is essential to the clock running at all. The pin must be the correct diameter — not too thick (binds in the slot) and not too thin (loose, intermittent engagement). It must be hard enough to resist deformation. It must be properly shaped and polished.

5. Look under magnification. The flat spot on the original pin was invisible at 5x. It was visible at 10x. Invest in good magnification and use it.

6. Bill Calder in Vermont asked me the right question. I want to acknowledge that. I had been looking at the escapement, the train, the strike — all the complex mechanisms — and the answer was in the simplest connector in the clock. Sometimes you need someone who doesn't know your case to ask the naive question.

There is no shame in asking for help. The clock doesn't care about your pride.

—H.J.

HAROLD JENNINGS — TECHNICAL NOTES
Subject: Common Mistakes — What I've Seen and What It Cost
Written for apprentice reference
414 Marietta Avenue, Lancaster, PA

---

These are real mistakes. Most of them I've watched an apprentice make. Some of them I made myself. I'm recording them not to embarrass anyone but because mistakes you understand are worth more than successes you can't explain.

I've changed the names in a few cases where the person is still working in the trade and would be embarrassed. I've left my own name on my own mistakes, of which there are several.

---

MISTAKE 1: STRIPPING A SCREW AND PROCEEDING ANYWAY

The clock: an Ansonia "Crystal Palace" parlor clock, circa 1890. Beautiful piece — visible brass movement behind beveled glass panels, eight-day, half-hour strike on a bell. Brought in by a woman whose grandmother had received it as a wedding gift.

The apprentice (second year, not yet named): encountered a movement plate screw that was tight. Applied more pressure. The slot began to strip. Applied more pressure. The head rounded. The movement plate could not be removed without further damage.

What should have happened: the moment resistance is encountered beyond what's normal, stop. Examine why. In this case the screw had a small amount of corrosion around the thread. The right tool was penetrating oil, time, and a screwdriver that fit the slot precisely. A screw slot that's slightly too small for your driver will always strip under pressure. The driver must fit the slot, not approximately fit.

The resolution: I extracted the screw with a screw extractor — a small reverse-thread bit that bites into the stripped head and removes it. The plate came off. We replaced the screw with a new one of correct dimensions. It cost an extra two hours and a moment of the customer's confidence.

The lesson: if a screw won't turn with reasonable pressure, the answer is not more pressure. The answer is more investigation.

MISTAKE 2: LUBRICATING THE WRONG SURFACES

The clock: a Sessions shelf clock from about 1915, simple time-only movement. Routine service.

The apprentice: had just learned about lubrication. Had been told "all pivot holes get oiled." Applied clock oil generously to every hole. Also, incorrectly, to the gear tooth faces.

Result: three months after service, the customer returned. The clock was running irregularly, gaining and losing time. Examination revealed that the oil had migrated from the gear faces onto the pallets, and from there onto the escape wheel teeth. Oil on pallet faces changes the friction characteristics of the escapement — the impulse was inconsistent.

What should have happened: oil goes in pivot holes only, in small quantities. Gear faces, wheel teeth, and pallet faces (except for a specific tiny amount of pallet oil on the pallet faces themselves — see escapement notes) do not get general clock oil.

The distinction between "lubrication points" and "surfaces that must stay dry" is essential. Get it wrong and the clock runs badly and you can't figure out why because everything looks right. The damage is invisible until you look under magnification.

The lesson: understand why each surface is lubricated or not lubricated. Don't generalize "lubrication is good." Specific lubrication in specific amounts at specific points. Everywhere else: dry.

MISTAKE 3: REASSEMBLING WITH A WORN BUSHING

The clock: a beautiful English longcase clock movement, early 18th century, eight-day with rack-and-snail strike. This was a valuable piece.

The apprentice (not in my shop — I heard this story from the clockmaker who had to fix it): During inspection, noticed visible wear on one of the going train pivot holes. The pivot had some play. Assessed it as minor and reassembled.

The clock ran. It ran fast. Over the next six months the worn bushing allowed the pivot to develop eccentric motion — the gear wasn't running truly centered in its pivot holes. This caused uneven gear meshing, which caused variable friction in the going train, which showed up as inconsistent timekeeping and, ultimately, accelerated wear on adjacent wheel teeth.

When the clock came back, the original minor bushing wear had become significant tooth wear on one wheel — a problem that now required the wheel to be replaced, at considerable cost on an 18th century movement.

What should have happened: worn bushings should be re-bushed during service. Full stop. Re-bushing is not difficult — it requires a bushing tool, appropriate bushings, and care. On a valuable old movement, if you're not certain of your re-bushing skills, you refer the work to someone who is.

The lesson: don't service a movement and leave known problems in it. If you found a worn bushing during inspection, address it. Telling a customer "I cleaned and oiled it but left the worn pivot hole" is not a complete service.

MISTAKE 4: BENDING A CLICK SPRING THE WRONG WAY — HAROLD JENNINGS, 1973

This one is mine.

I was servicing a Waterbury kitchen clock movement — a very common American movement type, straightforward work. The click spring (the spring that holds the click against the ratchet wheel) was slightly bent out of shape. I straightened it. In the process, I bent it in the wrong direction — instead of returning it to its original profile, I over-corrected and bent it past correct.

The result: the click spring applied too much tension against the click, making winding the clock stiff. You could wind it, but it was notably harder than it should be. Not a catastrophic problem. But the customer noticed and brought the clock back.

I knew immediately what I'd done. The repair was simple — a second, careful bend. But the conversation with the customer was awkward, and the extra time cost me an hour.

What I learned: when straightening any spring, understand its correct profile before you start. If you have a reference — the service manual, another identical movement, or a reference photograph — use it. If you don't have a reference, proceed slowly and incrementally. Bend a little, check, bend a little more.

Also: springs have memory only up to a point. A spring that's been significantly bent out of shape may not spring back to its original profile cleanly — it may need to be shaped, not just straightened. Know the difference.

MISTAKE 5: CONFUSING LEFT-HAND THREADS

Some clockwork, particularly older American and certain German movements, uses left-hand threads in specific locations. The cannon pinion on some movements has a left-hand thread (so that the running of the clock — which tries to unscrew normal threads — actually tightens it). Some hands nuts are left-hand threaded.

I watched an apprentice in 2005 attempt to remove what he thought was a stuck cannon pinion by applying progressively more force in the normal counterclockwise direction. He was actually tightening a left-hand thread.

No damage occurred, fortunately, because he felt increasing resistance and stopped. But this is one of those cases where stopping at increased resistance is what prevented a mistake from becoming a disaster.

Rule: before applying force to remove any fastener or fitting, determine whether it's standard or left-hand threaded. Visual examination often can't tell you — you need to know whether a given design uses left-hand threads in that location. Britten's and Goodrich both note which components in various movements use non-standard threads.

When in doubt: try to remove with light force in the normal direction. If it won't move and you feel it tighten slightly as you try, reverse direction.

MISTAKE 6: RUNNING A CLOCK WITHOUT A PENDULUM TO TEST IT

The clock: a German Black Forest mantel clock.

The situation: the apprentice had reassembled the movement and wanted to test the striking mechanism before hanging the pendulum. Reasonable impulse. Started the clock running by hand, let it run.

The problem: many striking mechanisms determine the strike count from the position of the hour hand. Without the pendulum running and controlling the rate, the clock "ran" at whatever speed the spring and gear train allowed — which was fast. The striking mechanism attempted to advance through the hour count at high speed and the rack failed to reset properly at the end of the sequence. A small spring broke.

What should have happened: test a clock with its pendulum. If you need to test the striking without waiting for it to run around to the striking point, advance the hands slowly by hand — minute hand only, and slowly — to the striking point and let it strike normally with the pendulum running.

Running a movement without its oscillator, except for very brief hand-testing of the gear train, causes the movement to run at a rate it was never designed for. Things break.

MISTAKE 7: NOT DOCUMENTING WHAT YOU FOUND

Not a mechanical mistake, but an important one.

Every movement I work on gets a note: date received, customer, presenting complaint, what I found during inspection, what I did, what I replaced, what I found and left because it was borderline, and what I recommended. This takes five minutes.

Three times in my career I've had a customer return with a clock and dispute the work I did. In two of those three cases, my documentation resolved the dispute — I could show exactly what I'd found, exactly what I'd done, and in one case the documentation showed that I'd noted a marginal part and recommended replacement and the customer had declined.

In the third case, I didn't have adequate documentation and I couldn't be certain whether I'd missed something or the customer was remembering incorrectly. I gave a partial refund rather than dispute it further. The cost was small. The reminder was lasting.

Document your work. Every job. Every finding. Even if you think it's a simple service and nothing will ever come of it.

---

FINAL NOTE

These are not the only mistakes possible. I could write a document ten times as long. What I want you to take from this is not a checklist of things to avoid — though avoid them — but a habit of mind.

Every time something doesn't go as expected: stop. Ask why. Understand before you proceed.

The clock is always telling you something. When it resists, when it fails, when it runs wrong — that's information. Respond to the information, not to your assumption of what should be happening.

The assumptions are yours. The physics are the clock's. The clock wins every argument.

—H.J.

HAROLD JENNINGS — TECHNICAL NOTES
Subject: Cleaning — Solutions, Methods, and the Reasons Behind Them
Written for apprentice reference
414 Marietta Avenue, Lancaster, PA

---

Cleaning is not the glamorous part of clock repair. It's also the most important part. I have seen beautifully done repairs fail because the cleaning was inadequate. I have also seen clocks that needed only cleaning — no repair at all — that had been sitting in other shops for months because the repairer was looking for a mechanical problem that didn't exist.

Before you repair, clean. If you clean properly and the problem persists, then you repair.

---

WHAT YOU'RE REMOVING

Old lubricant. After about ten years (in a normal environment), clock oil begins to oxidize. It thickens. It becomes less lubricating and more adhesive. Pivots begin to drag. The clock runs slow, then erratically, then stops. At this point the oil is no longer a lubricant — it's an abrasive holding fine metal particles from pivot wear in suspension. Continued running makes things worse.

Dirt and airborne particles. A clock movement is not sealed. Dust, fibers, and airborne particles enter and settle on every surface. In a dusty environment — a workshop, a country home, a kitchen — the accumulation over years is significant.

Corrosion products. Brass oxidizes slowly in air. The oxide is a powdery surface layer that interferes with gear meshing and pivot movement. Steel parts (click springs, springs) can rust, producing iron oxide. Cleaning removes loose corrosion products; deep pitting cannot be removed and indicates parts that may need replacement.

---

MY CLEANING SOLUTION

I've tried many commercial clock cleaning solutions over the years. Most work adequately. My preference, which I've used since the mid-1980s, is a diluted version of L&R #3 solution — a commercial cleaning concentrate used by watchmakers — mixed at about half the concentration recommended for watches. Full watch-strength concentration can strip lacquer from brass plates if left too long.

For rinsing: distilled water, then anhydrous isopropyl alcohol (99%, not the 70% from the drugstore — the water content in 70% isopropyl causes water spots and flash rust on steel parts).

If you can't get L&R: ammonia-based cleaning solutions work reasonably well for brass. I've made a serviceable solution from diluted household ammonia (1 part ammonia to 4 parts water) with a small amount of dish soap. This is a field expedient, not my preference, but it works in a pinch.

What I don't use: acetone (too aggressive, attacks some materials), anything petroleum-based as a primary cleaner (hard to rinse completely), commercial degreasers designed for automotive use (often leave a residue).

---

CLEANING PROCEDURE, STEP BY STEP

I'll describe my procedure and then explain the reasoning behind each step.

1. Disassemble the movement completely. Every wheel, every arbor, every bridge, every plate. Every click, click spring, cannon pinion, motion works part. Everything. I have seen "cleaning" that consisted of spraying solvent into an assembled movement. This is not cleaning. You cannot clean what you cannot reach, and in an assembled movement you cannot reach most of the critical surfaces.

Lay out the parts in order on a clean cloth. The order matters — you'll be reassembling this movement, and having the parts laid out in their logical sequence prevents errors. I use a tray with a white cloth that I can see small parts against. Light gray or tan is second-best; never dark colors.

2. Inspect before cleaning. Before anything goes in solution, look at every part. Use a loupe — minimum 5x, I prefer 7x for this stage. What am I looking for? Cracked or chipped wheel teeth. Bent arbors. Worn pivot ends (they should be smooth and rounded; wear makes them scored or tapered). Click springs that are bent or cracked. Worn pivot holes in the plates (the hole should be close-fitting around the pivot; if there's visible play, the bushing is worn). Broken jewels if the movement has jewels.

Note what you find. Write it down if necessary. This inspection tells you whether cleaning is sufficient or whether parts need replacement or repair before reassembly.

3. Cleaning solution soak. I put all metal parts except mainsprings in the cleaning solution. Not the pendulum bob (usually lead or a soft alloy that cleaning solution may damage). Not any parts with lacquered finishes that are meant to stay lacquered. Typical soak time: five minutes for a lightly soiled movement, up to fifteen for a movement with heavy dried lubricant.

Agitation helps: I use an old ultrasonic cleaner for most work now, which is more effective and faster than hand agitation. A soft brush — an old toothbrush works — can loosen stubborn deposits. Don't scrub pivot pivots with anything hard; they're polished to close tolerances and scratching them damages their bearing surface.

4. Rinse, first rinse. Distilled water, changing the water at least once. This removes the cleaning solution and loosened debris.

5. Rinse, alcohol rinse. Anhydrous isopropyl, which displaces the water and dries quickly. This prevents water spots on brass and, critically, flash rust on any steel parts. Flash rust on a click spring can develop in minutes in humid conditions.

6. Drying. Air dry on a clean cloth or paper. For steel parts, I sometimes use a heat gun on low to accelerate drying — not hot enough to anneal the steel, just warm. A watchmaker's dry box is useful here if you have one.

7. Post-cleaning inspection. Inspect again after cleaning. Sometimes cleaning reveals problems that weren't visible through the surface contamination. I've found hairline cracks in wheels, worn pivot ends that looked all right through the dirt. Post-cleaning is the definitive inspection.

---

SPECIAL CASES

Gilded parts: Many fine French and German movements have gilded brass plates or wheels. Full immersion in cleaning solution will remove gilding. For gilded parts, clean with a soft cloth dampened with cleaner, rinse carefully, dry immediately. You won't get them as clean as you would with a full soak, but you won't strip the gilding either.

Lacquered brass: Some decorative case components are lacquered. Cleaning solution may soften or remove lacquer. Clean with a damp cloth only, or accept that you may be stripping the lacquer and will need to re-lacquer. On movements (rather than case parts), I rarely encounter intentional lacquering, but it does occur.

Enamel-painted dials: Do not put painted dials in cleaning solution. Ever. Clean with a slightly damp cloth and let dry completely. Moisture behind an enamel dial can cause the enamel to crack.

Suspension springs: The thin steel spring that suspends the pendulum is easy to overlook. It needs cleaning too — suspended in the solution briefly, rinsed, and dried immediately. A rusty suspension spring will corrode through in time. Replacement suspension springs are inexpensive; it's worth replacing them at each service rather than cleaning an old one.

---

THE ULTRASONIC CLEANER: USEFUL BUT NOT MAGICAL

I acquired my first ultrasonic cleaner in 1993. Before that I cleaned everything by hand. I want to be clear: the ultrasonic is a tool, not a substitute for understanding.

What the ultrasonic does well: removes loosened deposits from surfaces that are hard to reach with a brush. Efficient for batch cleaning of small parts.

What the ultrasonic does not do: replace pre-soak for heavily soiled parts. Replace post-cleaning inspection. Clean in areas you haven't examined. The ultrasonic doesn't know what it's cleaning. You do.

I've seen movements that were "ultrasonically cleaned" but not inspected, not examined, not understood. They came to me still not running. The cleaning was technically thorough and diagnostically useless because the person cleaned without first asking why it wasn't running.

Cleaning is preparation for understanding. It is not a substitute for it.

---

A NOTE ON PACE

Cleaning a movement properly takes time. A simple American shelf clock, eight-day time and strike: I allow two to three hours for complete disassembly, cleaning, and inspection. A more complex movement — French, German, any multi-complication — might take a full day.

New apprentices often rush the cleaning because they want to get to the "real work" — the repair. I understand this impulse. I had it myself. But the cleaning is the real work. The repair follows from it. Rushing the cleaning is rushing the diagnosis, and rushing the diagnosis is how you miss things.

Take the time. The clock has been sitting in that attic for thirty years. It can wait another hour while you clean it properly.

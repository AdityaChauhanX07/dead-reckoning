THE PURPOSE OF AN ESCAPEMENT

Before anything else, understand what an escapement is for. It's not primarily about making the ticking sound, though that's a result. It's about controlled energy release.

A clock mainspring or weight contains significant stored energy. If you released that energy without control, the gear train would spin until the energy was exhausted — in seconds. The timekeeping element — the pendulum or balance wheel — would be irrelevant. You'd have a very fast, useless machine.

The escapement's job is to release that energy in small, controlled increments, one tooth at a time, at a rate controlled by the oscillator (pendulum or balance). The ticking sound is the sound of that controlled release — each tick is one tooth of the escape wheel passing. Each tock is another. The regularity of the ticking is the regularity of the timekeeping.

When I say "the escapement isn't working," I mean: the controlled energy release is failing in some way. Either it's releasing too fast (the clock runs fast), too slow (it runs slow), inconsistently (it gains and loses unpredictably), or not at all (it stops).

THE VERGE ESCAPEMENT

The verge is the oldest escapement in mechanical clockmaking. It dates to at least the late 13th century. You find it in the oldest surviving tower clocks and in many antique chamber clocks from the 15th through early 18th centuries. Some early American shelf clocks from colonial period also use verge escapements.

Physical description: A vertical arbor (the verge staff) with two flags, or pallets, projecting from it at roughly right angles and offset from each other by about 90-100 degrees. The escape wheel for a verge is a crown wheel — a wheel with teeth pointing roughly axially (perpendicular to the wheel's plane), not radially as in most gear wheels. The verge staff is horizontal, running parallel to the wheel axle, and the pallets catch on opposite sides of the crown wheel.

How it works: As the crown wheel turns, a tooth pushes against one pallet, rotating the verge staff (and the pendulum or foliot attached to it) in one direction. As the pallet rotates far enough, the tooth escapes — hence "escapement" — and the wheel jumps forward until the other pallet catches the opposite side of the crown wheel. The oscillator's momentum carries it past center, then returns it, and the cycle repeats.

The verge is what we call a "recoil" escapement. At the moment of unlocking, the crown wheel actually reverses direction very slightly before continuing forward. You can see this if you watch carefully: the escape wheel appears to stutter. This is not a defect — it's intrinsic to how the verge works. The recoil is real and it means the pendulum is receiving a push, then a slight counter-impulse, which is part of why verge clocks are generally less accurate than later designs.

Common problems with verge escapements:

1. Pallet engagement depth. The pallets need to engage the crown wheel teeth at a specific depth — enough to lock reliably, not so deep that the wheel can't escape. Too shallow: "set," where the wheel locks and won't unlock. The clock stops. Too deep: interference on the escape impulse, erratic running. Adjustment is made by bending the verge staff very carefully or by reshaping the pallet faces. This is not a job for beginners. The adjustments are small and the consequences of overcorrection are significant.

2. Crown wheel tooth damage. The crown wheel teeth are relatively fragile — they project axially and take a direct impact with each impulse. A chipped or bent tooth causes an irregular tick at that position and, if severe, can stop the clock. Inspect every tooth under magnification.

3. Verge staff wear. The verge pivots wear over centuries. A worn pivot means the staff has lateral play, which means the pallets can shift out of proper engagement. The effect is similar to pallet depth problems but inconsistent — it changes based on position and temperature. Pivot wear requires re-bushing or replacement, not adjustment.

4. Crown wheel wear. The wheel itself wears at the tooth faces over time. Worn tooth faces give an irregular impulse. This is harder to address — it may require wheel replacement, which on old clocks means finding a suitable replacement or having one made.

THE ANCHOR (RECOIL) ESCAPEMENT

The anchor escapement was developed in the late 17th century, attributed to various inventors, and it largely displaced the verge in quality clockwork by the 1700s. It's the escapement you'll find in the vast majority of longcase (grandfather) clocks, quality bracket clocks, and better American shelf clocks from 1800 onward.

Physical description: A pallet arbor carrying a forked "anchor" shaped pallet with two angled pallet faces — the entry pallet and the exit pallet. The escape wheel has pointed teeth. The anchor is positioned above the escape wheel with the pallets straddling it. As the pendulum swings, the anchor rocks back and forth, alternately releasing and catching escape wheel teeth.

Why it's better than the verge: The anchor escapement has a long pendulum relative to the clock's size (the verge needed a very short pendulum). Long pendulums are more accurate because small errors average out over a longer, slower swing. The anchor also has less recoil than the verge, meaning the gear train wastes less energy on backward motion.

The "recoil" in "recoil anchor": The standard anchor escapement does still recoil. At the moment of unlocking, the escape wheel tooth slides along the impulse face of the pallet and the tooth's backward force causes the escape wheel to recoil slightly. This is visible as a slight backward jump of the second hand in clocks with second hands. It's also audible — the tick sounds slightly "soft" rather than crisp.

Common problems with anchor escapements:

1. Pallet face wear. The locking faces and impulse faces of the pallets wear over time from contact with escape wheel teeth. Worn pallet faces first cause loss of accuracy, then erratic running, then stopping. Inspection requires magnification. Worn faces can sometimes be repaired by polishing and reshaping; severely worn pallets usually need replacement. New steel pallets can be fitted to the original arbor.

2. Escape wheel tooth damage. The teeth on anchor escapement wheels are typically more delicate than verge crown wheel teeth. Bent or chipped teeth cause irregularity at that position in the cycle. Careful inspection of every tooth under magnification is mandatory during service.

3. Depth of engagement. Like the verge, the anchor's pallets must engage the escape wheel at the correct depth. On the anchor, this is adjusted by moving the pallet arbor closer to or farther from the escape wheel — an adjustment that requires changing the arbor's position in its pivot holes, usually by re-bushing one or both holes at the new correct depth. This is precision work. Getting it wrong means the clock either "sets" (stops because pallets won't unlock) or "overbanks" (pallets swing past the escape wheel teeth entirely).

4. Bent or damaged crutch. The crutch connects the pallet arbor to the pendulum rod. A bent crutch causes the pendulum to swing asymmetrically, which causes the escapement to run at different rates on each side of the swing — the clock gains at some times and loses at others. This shows up as irregular timekeeping that doesn't respond normally to rate adjustment.

How I test an anchor escapement before reassembly: I mount the escapement in the frame and hang a test pendulum. I set it swinging by hand and watch it through at least thirty complete swings. I'm listening and watching for: even tick and tock (not tick...tock or tick-tock), no skipping (more than one tooth passing at a time), no setting (the pendulum swings but the wheel doesn't advance), no overbanking (the anchor swings completely to one side and the pallet comes off the wheel). If I hear or see any of these, I stop and investigate before proceeding.

THE DEADBEAT ESCAPEMENT

The deadbeat escapement is a refinement of the anchor, developed by George Graham around 1715. You find it in precision regulators, high-quality bracket clocks, and some better longcase clocks. It is called "deadbeat" because there is no recoil — the escape wheel does not move backward during the locking phase.

Physical description: Like the anchor, but with differently shaped pallet faces. The locking face of each pallet is curved on a radius centered on the pallet arbor. This means that when a tooth is resting on the locking face (the "dead" period), the tooth exerts no net force on the pallet — neither forward nor backward. The wheel simply stops. No recoil.

Why this matters for accuracy: In a recoil escapement, the gear train reverses briefly during each cycle. This means the gear train's friction and inertia are involved in every swing, slightly disturbing the pendulum. In a deadbeat escapement, the gear train truly stops during the locking phase and only influences the pendulum during the brief impulse. The result is better isochronism — the pendulum swings more consistently because it's disturbed less.

The deadbeat is harder to work on than the standard anchor. The pallet faces must be shaped precisely or the advantage is lost — slightly wrong locking face geometry and you get partial recoil that's worse than a proper recoil design. I've seen repair jobs where someone tried to "fix" a deadbeat escapement by polishing the pallet faces and accidentally created a partial recoil. The clock ran worse after the "repair."

Rule for deadbeat work: if you're not confident you can restore the correct locking face geometry, clean and lubricate only. Don't reshape the pallets unless you have a clear defect to correct and a clear plan for correcting it.

---

LUBRICATION FOR ESCAPEMENTS

Use clock pallet oil only. Not watch oil — clock oil is a different viscosity. Not 3-in-1 oil. Not WD-40, which is not a lubricant at all. Clock pallet oil, applied to the pallet faces only, in a quantity that you can barely see.

The escape wheel teeth do not get oiled. The pivots of the pallet arbor get oiled at the pivot holes — a tiny amount, placed in the hole, not on the pivot shaft.

Too much oil on pallet faces causes the oil to migrate to the escape wheel teeth, which then deposits on the impulse face and causes erratic impulse. I've diagnosed what looked like a pallet face geometry problem that turned out to be oil migration from over-lubrication at the pallets. The symptoms are almost identical.

---

FINAL NOTE

Escapements are the soul of the clock. When the escapement is right, the clock is right. Most timekeeping problems trace back here. Learn to diagnose escapement problems and you can diagnose the majority of what you'll encounter.

Take your time here. I did not understand escapements properly until I'd worked on probably fifty of them. Don't be discouraged by that. The understanding accumulates.

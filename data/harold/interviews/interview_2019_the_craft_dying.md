INTERVIEW TRANSCRIPT — HAROLD JENNINGS
Subject: Late Reflections; The Shop Today; What He Hopes Survives
Recorded at Harold's workshop, 414 Marietta Avenue, Lancaster, PA
Interviewer: Sarah Okafor
Duration: 1 hour 28 minutes
[Harold is 79 in this interview. His hands show the first signs of arthritis but he is still working full days.]

---

Sarah: How's the shop right now?

Harold: Busy. Which still surprises me, honestly. I expected that by this point in my life, the work would be slowing down. Instead I have a four-month wait. There seem to be more people interested in mechanical clocks, not fewer. I think it's a reaction to everything being digital. People want something they can see working. Something with moving parts. I understand that impulse.

What's changed is the nature of the work that comes in. Twenty years ago most of what I saw was American clocks — Ansonia, Seth Thomas, New Haven, Waterbury. Good solid movements, usually straightforward problems. Now I see more European work, more complicated pieces, more things that people have purchased at estate sales or inherited without knowing what they have. Last week I had a complicated pocket watch movement come in that someone had found in their grandfather's desk. English, I think, probably 1870s, with a repeater mechanism. The person had no idea what it was. They called it "a watch with extra parts."

Sarah: Are you still enjoying it?

Harold: [long pause] Yes. Not every day — there are days when my hands don't cooperate and I can feel the difference between what I want to do and what I can do. That's new. But the work itself, yes. I still have days when I get to the bench at seven and look up and it's three in the afternoon and I've been inside a movement for eight hours and it felt like nothing.

That absorption — I don't know what to call it except a gift. Raymond had it. I have it. Not every clockmaker has it. You either find the work completely compelling or you find it tedious, and those are different people even if they can technically do the same tasks.

Sarah: What keeps you up at night, professionally?

Harold: [immediately] The orphaned clocks.

There are clocks in this shop right now — good clocks, beautiful clocks — that I'm repairing for people who won't maintain them. They'll come back in fifteen years when the oil has dried and the pivots have worn, and by then more damage will have been done. A clock that's serviced every ten years is a clock that will run for two hundred more years. A clock that's only serviced when it stops — and stops badly — might not make it another fifty.

I tell every customer: bring it back in eight to ten years for a cleaning even if it seems fine. Maybe one in ten does. The rest are waiting for something to go wrong.

The other thing that keeps me up is what I'll call the parts problem. When I started, you could get replacement parts — new click springs, new clicks, bushings, mainsprings — for virtually any American clock movement. The supply houses were well stocked and the tradition of repair was strong enough that there was commercial incentive to maintain the supply. That's eroding. Some of what I need now I have to make myself or have made, or salvage from donor movements. In another twenty years, I think the parts problem is going to be severe for anyone who wants to properly restore older clocks rather than fit modern substitute parts. And modern substitute parts are almost never as good.

Sarah: If you could preserve one thing — one specific piece of knowledge — what would it be?

Harold: [thinks for a very long time] 

The feel of a correctly running train.

You can describe it. I've tried. The train runs freely, the resistance is even and smooth, there's no catch or hesitation, the escapement sounds like — it's hard to put into words, it has a quality, a crispness to the tick. But the description isn't the thing. The thing is in the hands. It's in the comparison between what you feel now and the ten thousand other trains you've felt, and the accumulated sense of what's right.

Every problem I've ever diagnosed started with something not feeling the way it should. Not a dramatic failure — usually just a subtle deviation from what a correctly running movement feels like. That sensitivity, that reference point, is built over years and years of handling movements. I don't know how you preserve that.

Maybe you can't. Maybe some things can only be transmitted in person, hand to hand, bench to bench. Maybe that's not a tragedy — maybe that's just the nature of some knowledge.

But I hope I'm wrong.

Sarah: Is there anything you want people who come after you to know?

Harold: [long pause, looking at his bench]

Work slowly enough to understand what you're doing. The speed will come.

The clock has its own logic. Before you try to make it do what you want, understand what it was designed to do. Most problems come from someone not respecting the original design — using the wrong oil, fitting a part that's close but not correct, making an adjustment without understanding what the adjustment is for.

Ask questions. Ask them of people who know more than you. Ask them of the clock. The clock is always telling you something.

And — this sounds sentimental but I believe it — remember that these objects came from someone's hands. Someone cut those wheels, crossed those pinions, calculated those gear ratios and that pendulum length. Before you, there was a craftsman who made this thing to keep time, to be used, to last. You are part of a long chain of people who have cared for it. That's not a small thing to be.

Take care of the things in your care.

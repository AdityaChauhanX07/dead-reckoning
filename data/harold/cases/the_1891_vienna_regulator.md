HAROLD JENNINGS — CASE STUDY
Subject: The 1891 Vienna Regulator — A Restoration in Three Acts
Written from memory and bench notes, 2010
414 Marietta Avenue, Lancaster, PA

---

I keep notes on every job I do, but some jobs I also write up as narratives, because the story of the diagnosis is worth more than the list of what I did. This is one of those.

---

ACT ONE: ARRIVAL

The clock came to me in April 2001 through an unusual chain of custody. A woman named Patricia Holtz had inherited it from her uncle, who had purchased it from an estate in Vienna in 1972 and shipped it to Philadelphia, where it sat in his study until his death. The uncle was not a clockmaker; he had admired the piece, had it set up by someone whose name Patricia didn't know, and it had run intermittently for thirty years.

Patricia herself knew nothing about clocks. She brought it to me in two parts — the movement in a padded box, the case leaning against the back seat of her car wrapped in a blanket. She said it had stopped about eight months ago and she'd thought about selling it but hadn't been able to bring herself to.

The movement was a Vienna Regulator, one-weight, almost certainly late 19th century Viennese manufacture, with a deadbeat escapement, seconds pendulum, and a beautifully made brass movement with maintaining power. The maintaining power is the feature that always signals a quality instrument — it's a mechanism that keeps the clock running during winding, so that the timekeeping isn't disrupted by the act of winding. Lesser clocks don't have it.

The presenting complaint: stopped. Had been running slowly before it stopped, the customer thought, though she wasn't certain.

My initial assessment, looking at the movement before disassembly: the movement was dusty but not catastrophically dirty. The previous "repairer" had done something — I could see fresh oil at the pivot holes, relatively recent, possibly within the last decade. But the application was uneven and there was definitely old oil in several locations, which told me the service had been incomplete. Some parts had been cleaned and oiled, others left. This is worse than no service, in some ways — it introduces inconsistency.

The escape wheel looked right at a glance. The deadbeat pallet faces looked intact. I wound the movement partially by hand and tried to start it. It started, ran for about thirty seconds, and stopped. I tried again. Forty seconds, stopped.

Note: when a clock runs briefly and then stops, rather than not running at all, this is valuable diagnostic information. Something is working and then failing, rather than being broken outright.

---

ACT TWO: DIAGNOSIS

Disassembly. Full cleaning per my normal procedure. Post-cleaning inspection of every part.

What I found during inspection:

1. One worn bushing in the going train — third wheel, back plate. Significant enough to address.

2. The click spring was slightly weak — it was holding the click against the ratchet, but with less tension than it should have. This wasn't the cause of stopping but would need addressing.

3. The maintaining power mechanism: all parts present and intact. But the maintaining spring (a small spring that drives the maintaining mechanism during winding) was set — had taken a permanent curve that reduced its effective tension. It was still functional but marginal.

4. The deadbeat pallets: here I spent a long time.

Under magnification, the locking faces looked correct. But the impulse faces showed something subtle: there was a tiny area of unevenness on the entry pallet impulse face — not a crack, not obvious wear, but a slight change in surface finish that suggested either very fine wear or a previous repair attempt that hadn't blended perfectly. This was the kind of finding where you second-guess yourself. Was I seeing something real or was I looking too hard?

I set that question aside and continued the inspection. I would return to the pallet faces.

5. The pendulum rod: slightly bent. Very slightly — I almost missed it. The rod is steel, about 100cm long for a seconds pendulum, and it had a gentle bow to it. A bent pendulum rod can cause the pendulum to swing in a slightly elliptical path rather than a clean arc, which introduces variables into the escapement engagement.

I believed I had my diagnosis: the slightly bent pendulum rod was causing the pendulum to swing inconsistently, which combined with the borderline pallet face irregularity to produce occasional escapement failure. Not every swing — hence the clock running briefly before stopping — but often enough to arrest the movement.

---

ACT THREE: THE REPAIR AND WHAT I LEARNED

I re-bushed the third wheel pivot hole. I replaced the click spring. I replaced the maintaining power spring.

The pendulum rod: I straightened it carefully, using a steel block as a reference surface and adjusting the rod until it lay flat along its full length. Pendulum rod straightening is patience work — you make a small correction, check, make another, check. The rod needs to be straight within, I'd estimate, about a quarter millimeter over its full length. Close enough to straight is not straight enough.

The pallet faces: I decided to polish them very lightly with a hard Arkansas stone and then with peg wood — a very fine final polish. The risk was altering the geometry; the reason was that the surface irregularity I'd seen was almost certainly contributing to inconsistent impulse. I worked slowly, testing after each pass, reassembling the escapement and hand-testing the action.

After the first polish pass: still some irregularity in feel.
After the second: better.
After the third: I felt what I'd been looking for — the impulse had a clean, even quality through the full swing arc.

Reassembled the movement. Let it run for four days on my bench before calling Patricia.

The result: the clock ran for four days without stopping. The beat was even. I regulated it against a reference clock and by day four it was keeping time to within six seconds per day — which for a regulator that had been dormant and partially serviced is not its ultimate accuracy, but respectable. With fine regulation over weeks it should do better.

Patricia came to collect it. I explained what I'd found and done. She asked if it would last. I told her that with proper service every eight to ten years, it should outlast both of us.

She sent me a note three years later to tell me it was still running.

---

WHAT THIS CASE TAUGHT ME

1. An incomplete previous service can be harder to diagnose than no previous service. When some things have been recently oiled and others haven't, the evidence you'd normally use to date the service is confused.

2. A clock that runs briefly and then stops is giving you a clue. The fault is intermittent or marginal, not catastrophic. Look for things that are almost right rather than things that are obviously wrong.

3. The pendulum rod is easy to overlook because it looks simple — it's a straight rod with a bob. But a bent rod introduces asymmetry into the pendulum's swing, which compounds any marginal problem in the escapement. Check the rod. Check it against a flat reference surface, not by eye.

4. Deadbeat pallet face irregularity is subtle and worth spending time on. The impulse quality of a deadbeat escapement is its whole value — if the impulse isn't clean, the clock won't keep good time even if nothing is obviously wrong.

5. Full diagnosis before repair. I could have rushed to re-bush the pivot hole and replace the click spring and called it done. The clock might have run for a while before the real problem arrested it again. Taking the time to examine every part meant I found the pallet irregularity and the bent rod — the actual causes rather than the incidental findings.

The clock doesn't know you meant well. But it rewards patience.

—H.J.

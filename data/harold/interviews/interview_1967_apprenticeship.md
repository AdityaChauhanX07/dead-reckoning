INTERVIEW TRANSCRIPT — HAROLD JENNINGS
Subject: Early Years, Apprenticeship Under Raymond Kessler, 1967–1972
Recorded at Harold's workshop, 414 Marietta Avenue, Lancaster, PA
Interviewer: Sarah Okafor (apprentice, age 24)
Duration: 1 hour 42 minutes

---

Sarah: Harold, can you tell me how you got started? Like, the very beginning?

Harold: The very beginning. [laughs softly] Lord, that's going back. I was seventeen. My father — his name was Walter Jennings, most people called him Walt — he had a small watch repair counter at the back of a jewelry store on Prince Street. Not clocks, watches mostly. Hamiltons, Elgins, a few Swiss pieces. He was good at watches but he didn't have the patience for clocks. He always said clocks were a different animal. He meant it as a warning. I took it as an invitation.

There was a man named Raymond Kessler who had a proper clock shop on West Lemon Street. He'd been there since 1951. Before that he apprenticed in Philadelphia under a German-trained clockmaker whose name I never learned — Raymond only ever called him "the German," never with any disrespect, just that's how he referred to him. Raymond was sixty-one when I walked into his shop. I was there to buy a replacement mainspring for a French mantel clock I'd gotten at an estate sale and was trying to fix on my own. Foolishly. [pause] I'd already broken the click spring trying to release the power before I understood what I was doing. Raymond took one look at what I'd brought in and he didn't laugh. That's the first thing I noticed. He just laid the parts out on his bench and said, "Sit down. I'll show you what you did wrong."

I sat. He showed me. I came back the next Saturday. And the one after that.

Sarah: He just let you come back?

Harold: I asked if I could. He didn't say yes exactly. He said, "Don't touch anything and don't ask questions while I'm working." That was his version of yes. I followed those rules for four months before he let me hold a tool.

Sarah: Four months just watching?

Harold: Watching and listening. He talked to himself while he worked, which is a habit I picked up from him and never lost. He'd say things like, "Here's where the amateur makes his mistake," or "You see how the wear is on this side? That tells you something." He wasn't talking to me directly but he wasn't not talking to me either. It was like being allowed to listen to someone think.

The first thing he let me do — this was February of 1968, so about five months in — was clean a movement. A Seth Thomas mantel clock, eight-day, time and strike. Simple American movement. He had me disassemble it, put every piece in the cleaning solution, rinse it, dry it, lay it all out in order. It took me the whole morning. When I was done he came over and looked at the layout and he pointed to three parts and said, "What are these?" I knew what they were. I'd been reading. He nodded and walked away. That was the whole exchange. But I understood that I'd passed something.

Sarah: What was the hardest part of learning in those early years?

Harold: Learning to feel things. That sounds strange but it's the truth. Raymond had this saying: "Your eyes will lie to you. Your hands won't." What he meant is that visually, a gear train can look right — the wheels are meshing, there's no obvious damage. But if you spin the train by hand and you feel a hesitation, a catch, a place where the resistance changes slightly, that tells you something your eyes can't. A pivot that's slightly bent. A worn jewel that's not seating properly. A click spring that's just barely dragging.

It took me almost two years to develop that feeling. There were times I'd hand Raymond a movement I thought was perfect and he'd take it in his hands, spin the train twice, and hand it back to me. He never said much. Just "again." I'd go back to the bench and I'd feel like I'd missed nothing, and I'd look for an hour and finally find it — a pivot that wasn't quite polished, a click that had the tiniest burr. You learn to feel things because you have no choice.

Sarah: Did you ever just fail completely? Like, ruin something?

Harold: [long pause] Yes. Once badly enough that I'm still not entirely at peace with it.

This was 1969. Raymond had given me a longcase clock — what most people call a grandfather clock, though Raymond despised that term — to work on unsupervised for the first time. It was a beautiful piece. English, probably 1780s, eight-day, rack and snail strike mechanism. Belonged to a family in Lititz who'd had it for four generations. The problem was that the strike was running fast — it was striking the wrong number of hours.

I diagnosed the problem correctly, which was that the rack tail wasn't engaging the snail properly. I corrected that. What I didn't do was check the click work carefully enough before reassembling. There was a worn click spring that I'd noted and decided wasn't worn enough to replace. I was wrong. Two days after the customer picked it up, the clock ran down and when it did, the unchecked power release damaged the great wheel. Not catastrophically, but enough that the tooth spacing was compromised. The clock had to come back in. Raymond fixed it. He charged the customer nothing extra and took it out of my pay, which was fair.

But what I remember isn't the money or even Raymond's reaction, which was very quiet and very pointed. What I remember is the customer's face when he brought it back. This was his great-great-grandfather's clock. He'd trusted us with it. I had been careless with something irreplaceable.

Raymond said something to me that day that I've repeated probably five hundred times in my own teaching. He said: "The clock doesn't know you meant well." The clock doesn't know you meant well. It will fail according to what you did, not according to what you intended.

Sarah: That's stayed with you.

Harold: Every day. Every time I'm tempted to say "that's probably fine" instead of checking.

Sarah: What did a normal workday look like, learning under Raymond?

Harold: I arrived at eight. Raymond was always there before me. He started at seven, which I didn't know for the first year — I just assumed he arrived at eight like I did. Then I came in one day at seven because I'd forgotten something and he was already two hours into a movement. That was instructive.

We worked until five-thirty or six. Raymond took a twenty-minute lunch, ate at his bench, never at a restaurant. He had a thermos of coffee his wife made. I never met his wife — she died before I started. He spoke of her occasionally. Her name was Helen.

The work itself — you learn the rhythm of it. Disassembly, cleaning, inspection, repair, reassembly, testing, regulation. Those stages are always the same. What changes is what you find in the inspection. A good clockmaker is really a diagnostician. You're looking for what failed, yes, but more importantly you're looking for why. Because if a mainspring broke, you need to know if it broke because it was old or because something was binding the barrel. If something was binding the barrel, you haven't fixed the clock by putting in a new mainspring.

Raymond had a phrase for that too: "Don't treat the symptom." He said it about clocks and I've always thought he was talking about more than clocks.

Sarah: Do you think the trade has changed since then?

Harold: [very long pause] Changed. Yes. Whether it's changed in ways that matter — that's harder to say.

The tools are better in some respects. Ultrasonic cleaners, better gauges, materials that weren't available. The reference materials are better — there are databases now, collectors' groups, information that Raymond had to carry in his head or find in books that were out of print.

What's changed that worries me is the patience. Not in every young person — you're here, you came back, you're doing the work. But the culture doesn't reward patience the way it once did. Raymond waited four months before he let me touch a tool. Today, people find that difficult to understand. Everything is supposed to be accessible immediately. A clock movement is not accessible immediately. It will not be rushed. The gear train has been meshing at the same rate for two hundred years and it doesn't care about your timeline.

The other thing that's changed is the supply of work. When I started in this trade, if a clock stopped, you took it to a clockmaker. Now many people throw it away. That's a kind of cultural poverty that I find genuinely sad. There are clocks in this shop right now — beautiful, intricate objects made by craftsmen a century and a half ago — that were nearly thrown in the trash by people who didn't know what they had.

Sarah: Last question for today — what made you decide to take on apprentices yourself? You didn't have to.

Harold: [pause] Raymond died in 1981. Heart attack, sitting at his bench. Which is how he would have wanted to go, I think. His shop closed. There was no one to take it over — he had a son but the son wasn't interested.

After Raymond died I thought about what would have happened to me if he hadn't said "sit down" that afternoon in 1967. I'd have gone home with my broken French clock and probably made it worse. Maybe I'd have become a plumber or a schoolteacher. Who knows. But he said sit down, and that changed things.

I started taking apprentices in 1985. I've had eleven. Some stayed in the trade, some didn't. But every one of them can feel a gear train. That's something.

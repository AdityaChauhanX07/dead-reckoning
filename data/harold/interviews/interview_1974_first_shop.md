INTERVIEW TRANSCRIPT — HAROLD JENNINGS
Subject: Opening the Lancaster Shop, 1974–1982, Early Customers
Recorded at Harold's workshop, 414 Marietta Avenue, Lancaster, PA
Interviewer: Sarah Okafor
Duration: 1 hour 18 minutes

---

Sarah: Tell me about opening your own shop.

Harold: I was twenty-four. Which in retrospect is very young to be running a business, though I didn't know that at the time. I'd been working for Raymond for seven years. I was good enough — Raymond said so, which from him was considerable praise. He wasn't given to flattery. But I wanted my own bench. My own hours. My own way of organizing things.

Raymond organized his bench in a way I could never fully adapt to. He kept everything in small wooden trays that he'd made himself, very specific placement — balance wheels here, mainsprings there, click springs in their own drawer by size. His system worked perfectly for him and I could never internalize it. I developed my own system over those years and I kept having to translate between his system and mine. Having my own shop meant I could stop translating.

I found the space on Marietta Avenue through a customer, actually. A man named Gideon Stoltzfus — Old Order Mennonite, came in twice a year with clocks from his community. You might wonder why a traditionalist community would have mechanical clocks needing repair, but of course they do — mechanical clocks require no electricity. Gideon was quiet, very precise in how he described a problem, always brought the clock wrapped in a clean cloth. He mentioned that his brother-in-law had vacated a small commercial space on Marietta. The rent was ninety dollars a month. I signed a lease in November of 1973 and opened January 2nd, 1974.

Sarah: What did the shop look like?

Harold: Small. One room in the front for receiving customers, my workbench in the back. I had a glass case for displaying a few clocks I'd acquired and restored. The bench I built myself from plans in a woodworking book — it's not the bench I have now, this one I built in 1989, but the dimensions are almost identical. I knew what I needed by then.

The first month was very slow. I had some customers who followed me from Raymond's, which was generous of them — Raymond didn't discourage it, which was more generous still. But mostly I spent that January working on clocks I'd bought at estate sales specifically to have something to do. I restored a beautiful New Haven kitchen clock that month. Black walnut case, time and alarm. No customer waiting for it, just me and the movement.

Sarah: Do you remember your first real customer?

Harold: I do. Her name was Dorothy Hauptmann. She came in with a cuckoo clock, which I admit gave me a small internal sigh, because cuckoo clocks are not my favorite thing to work on. They're not uninteresting mechanically but they attract a particular kind of sentimental attachment that makes customers difficult. Everything about a cuckoo clock is "it was my grandmother's" or "it came from Germany before the war." That's not nothing, but it makes the conversation complicated.

Dorothy's clock was a Black Forest piece, probably 1920s, eight-day with music. The cuckoo mechanism wasn't the problem — the problem was the strike train, which had a worn cam. Simple enough repair. But Dorothy sat in the chair I had for customers and talked to me for forty-five minutes. About where the clock came from, about her grandmother's house in Harrisburg, about the sound it made when she was a child.

I understood then that the work isn't just mechanical. People bring you objects that are vessels of time — not clock time, but their time. Their memories. A clock that belonged to someone's grandmother isn't just a clock anymore. It's an obligation.

I charged Dorothy eight dollars for the repair. She gave me ten and told me to keep it. She sent me three customers in the next month.

Sarah: What were the most common problems you saw in those early years?

Harold: Mainspring failure, mostly. Neglect. In the 1970s there were a lot of clocks that had sat in attics or basements for twenty, thirty years — post-war prosperity had meant people bought new, and the old clocks got pushed aside. Many of those movements had dried lubricant. When you have old lubricant it doesn't just stop lubricating — it becomes an abrasive. The pivots wear faster, the jewels wear faster, the bushings wear. A clock that ran perfectly in 1940 and then sat in an attic until 1975 is in much worse shape than a clock that ran continuously, because at least running continuously circulates what oil there is.

The remedy is the same as it's always been: a proper cleaning, new oil at every friction point, and then patience to see what's actually worn versus what just needed cleaning.

Sarah: Were there repairs in those years that really challenged you?

Harold: The first Vienna Regulator I ever worked on. This was 1976. A customer named Peter Albrecht brought it in — he was a professor at Franklin and Marshall, German literature. He'd acquired the clock in Vienna, actually, which gave it a kind of double provenance. It was a one-weight Regulator, probably 1850s, with a deadbeat escapement and a very fine seconds pendulum. Absolutely beautiful instrument.

The problem was the escapement. Deadbeat escapements are precise but unforgiving — the geometry of the pallet faces has to be exact or the clock will gain or lose unpredictably. This one was losing about four minutes a day, which for a Regulator is a significant error. These clocks were made to be accurate to seconds per week.

I spent three days on that movement. The pallets needed refinishing, which I'd never done on a deadbeat — I'd done anchor escapements, which are more forgiving, but not deadbeat. I got out every reference I had, which wasn't much. I wrote a letter to the National Association of Watch and Clock Collectors asking if anyone could advise me. I got a reply from a man in Ohio who sent me two pages of handwritten notes. I still have those notes somewhere.

The work itself was done under magnification with polishing sticks — you're refinishing the locking faces and the impulse faces to extremely close tolerances. A few thousandths of an inch of error will show up in the timekeeping. I must have done it four times before the clock ran at under thirty seconds a day. Then a few more adjustments and I had it to under ten. Professor Albrecht came to pick it up and I told him what I'd done and he looked at me and said, "Do you understand what this clock meant to its original owner? To someone in Vienna in 1855, accurate time was a luxury." I've thought about that often.

Sarah: How did you learn things when there was no one to ask?

Harold: You read everything you can find. You write letters — I wrote a great many letters in the 1970s, to collectors, to the NAWCC, to suppliers. You make careful mistakes: you do the work, you document what you did, you observe the result, you adjust. And you develop humility about the things you don't know.

Raymond had a rule that I've kept: if you're not sure, don't proceed until you are sure. It's better to tell a customer you need another week than to reassemble a movement incorrectly and have them bring it back. The second conversation is always harder than the first.

Sarah: Tell me about a mistake you made in those years.

Harold: [pause] There was a Black Forest shield clock — a Schilduhr, very traditional style, painted wooden case, around 1830. The owner brought it in for general servicing. When I cleaned the movement I found that one of the wheel teeth had a chip — a small one, maybe thirty percent of the tooth height. It wasn't in the engagement zone. I made a judgment that it was stable and didn't require intervention.

Six months later the wheel shed that tooth completely. The clock stopped. Worse, when the train arrested suddenly it put stress on the arbor and bent it slightly. Now a simple chip had become a wheel replacement and an arbor straightening.

What I should have done was either repair the tooth — there are techniques, silver soldering, careful filing — or recommend the wheel be replaced preventively. Instead I made an optimistic judgment without enough basis for optimism. I've thought about it as a calibration error. I hadn't yet developed good judgment about what would hold and what would fail. That takes years of seeing failures.

Now I have a rule: if I see damage and I'm not certain it's stable, I tell the customer. I give them the choice. "This tooth has a chip. It may be fine for years, it may fail next month. I can leave it or I can address it. Here's the cost of each option." The customer decides with full information.

The clock doesn't know you meant well. Raymond said that. I find new applications for it regularly.

Sarah: By 1982 you were well established?

Harold: Established enough. I had a waiting list, which is a strange kind of problem to have. Too much work is better than too little but it puts pressure on you. I'd hired a part-time helper — a young man named Carl who did cleaning and ran errands — but the skilled work was all mine.

My wife Dorothy — different Dorothy than the cuckoo clock customer — she said I worked too much. She wasn't wrong. But I was in the middle of something that I found genuinely, completely absorbing. I've never been able to fully explain to someone who isn't a clockmaker what it feels like to take a movement that has been stopped for thirty years, understand why it stopped, remedy that, and hear it start running again. There's a sound a clock makes when it's running properly — the tick is even, the train is smooth — that is, I think, one of the better sounds in the world.

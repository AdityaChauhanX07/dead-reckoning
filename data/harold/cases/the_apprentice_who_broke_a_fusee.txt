HAROLD JENNINGS — CASE STUDY / TEACHING STORY
Subject: The Apprentice Who Broke the Fusee Chain — And What We Both Learned
Written 2008
414 Marietta Avenue, Lancaster, PA

---

In 1998 I had an apprentice named David Chen who was in his third year with me. David was gifted — better than I was at his stage, honestly. Quick mind, good hands, genuine love of the work. He's running his own shop in Philadelphia now and he's better than most.

But in 1998 he broke a fusee chain, and how he broke it and what happened afterward is worth recording.

---

THE FUSEE

Many apprentices encounter fusee movements relatively late in their learning because fusee mechanisms are found primarily in English bracket clocks and high-quality American shelf clocks, not in the more common American movements you work on in early training.

The fusee is an ingenious device for equalizing mainspring power. As I described in the mainspring notes, a mainspring delivers more power when fully wound and less when nearly run down. For fine timekeeping, you want as even a power delivery as possible.

The fusee solves this with elegance: it's a cone-shaped pulley with a helical groove. A chain (or gut line) connects the fusee to the mainspring barrel. When the spring is fully wound and delivering maximum force, the chain is on the narrow end of the cone — mechanically disadvantaged, requiring more force from the spring to turn the gear. As the spring runs down and its force diminishes, the chain migrates toward the wider end of the cone — mechanically advantaged, requiring less force to turn the gear. The varying mechanical advantage of the cone compensates for the varying force of the spring. Result: relatively even torque delivered to the gear train throughout the running cycle.

The chain is thin steel links. It must be precisely made — the chain length, the link size, and the groove spacing of the fusee must match exactly. Replacement chains are available for common fusee sizes but are not universal; a mismatched chain will not transfer power correctly.

---

WHAT HAPPENED

David was servicing a fine English bracket clock — Georgian period, approximately 1780, eight-day fusee movement with pull-quarter repeat. The pull repeat mechanism allows the owner to pull a cord and hear the clock repeat the last quarter hour — useful in a bedroom before electric light. This was a significant piece, belonging to a collector who'd paid a meaningful sum for it.

The clock had been fully cleaned and was being reassembled. David was winding the fusee to set tension — a step that requires care because you're loading the mainspring through the fusee mechanism, and the chain is under tension.

He felt resistance. Normal resistance — there's always resistance when winding a fusee. He continued winding.

The resistance increased. He continued.

The chain broke.

---

THE CONVERSATION

I was at my bench six feet away. I heard a small, distinct sound that I recognized — the particular sound of a fusee chain under tension giving way — and I put down what I was in and walked over.

David was standing at his bench looking at the movement. The broken chain was in two pieces. His face was the color of chalk.

I didn't say anything immediately. I looked at the break. Then I looked at the fusee. Then I looked at the click mechanism.

Here's what had happened: during reassembly, the maintaining power mechanism (this clock had maintaining power) had been assembled in a position that caused it to lock against the fusee rotation. When David wound the fusee, the chain was trying to rotate the barrel against the locked maintaining power. The chain, being the weakest element in that load path, broke.

The maintaining power assembly order for this movement required a specific sequence — maintain power spring set to a specific position before the fusee and barrel were engaged. The service manual for this movement type — which I have, and which was on the shelf — specified this sequence. David had not consulted it.

I asked him: "What were you thinking when you felt increased resistance?"

He said: "I thought it was normal."

I asked: "How did you know it was normal?"

He said: "I assumed."

That's the sentence. I assumed. He assumed the increased resistance was normal because he expected some resistance and didn't have a reference for what abnormal resistance felt like. He hadn't consulted the manual because he felt confident in his knowledge of the assembly. He was mostly right about the assembly — except for one specific step that the manual would have told him.

---

THE REPAIR

The chain break was repairable, fortunately. I have a supply of fusee chain in several sizes and this movement's chain size was one I stocked. We spliced a new section into the chain using the traditional method — link by link, with fine chain-nose pliers, under magnification.

The repair took two hours. David did the work under my supervision. It was important that he do the work, not watch me do it. Repairing the damage he'd caused was part of the lesson.

We also had to carefully reset the maintaining power mechanism in the correct sequence per the manual. The clock ran correctly after reassembly.

The collector was told that the service had taken longer than anticipated due to a difficulty with the maintaining power mechanism. This was true, if incomplete. I absorbed the extra time cost. David's pay for that week reflected the two hours, minus what I typically charged for this type of job — he paid for the chain from his wages, which was fair.

---

THE LESSON, IN FULL

I sat with David at the end of that day and we talked through what happened. Not angrily — there was nothing to be angry about, it was a learning event. But completely.

I told him:

When you feel unexpected resistance, that's information. Resistance that's greater than expected means something in your model of the situation is wrong. The question is: what?

You had two choices at the moment of unexpected resistance: proceed, or stop and investigate. You chose to proceed. Why?

He said: because the resistance seemed like it might be normal.

I said: how would you have known the difference?

He didn't have an answer.

The answer is: you often can't tell from feel alone. You need a reference. The reference might be your experience with identical movements. The reference might be the service manual. The reference might be asking someone who knows. In this case, the service manual was on the shelf three feet from his bench.

The rule I gave him then, and that I've given every apprentice since:

Unexpected resistance is a stop signal, not a proceed-with-caution signal. Stop. Investigate. Understand why the resistance is there before continuing. If you can't explain the resistance, you don't know enough to proceed safely.

A fusee chain costs a few dollars and an hour of repair time. Some mistakes cost more. The rule applies equally to all of them.

---

APPENDIX: ABOUT FUSEE CHAINS

Since this case involves a fusee, some technical notes for reference.

Fusee chain types: modern chains are usually sold in sizes referenced by their width and pin diameter. Older movements may have unusual sizes not in current production, requiring custom work or careful salvage from donor movements. Measure carefully before ordering a replacement.

Chain end attachments: one end of the chain attaches to the fusee (usually via a click arrangement at the top of the fusee), and the other to the barrel (usually via a small hole or hook). Both ends must be secure. A loose end connection causes the chain to jump off under load.

Chain inspection: examine every link under magnification during service. A cracked or deformed link will fail under load. A chain with a broken link that's been crimped back together by a previous repairer should be replaced entirely — the repair is never as strong as the original and fails eventually.

Gut line alternative: some fusee movements use gut line (actually a fine synthetic cord now) rather than metal chain. Gut/cord stretches slightly with age, which affects the fusee geometry and power equalization. Inspect gut lines carefully and replace them routinely in service rather than waiting for failure.

Winding sequence: for fusee movements with maintaining power, always consult the movement's documentation for the correct assembly and winding sequence. This sequence matters. Consult it every time, even if you think you know it.

David Chen consults it every time now. He's told me so.

INTERVIEW TRANSCRIPT — HAROLD JENNINGS
Subject: Teaching Philosophy Deep Dive; The Future of the Craft
Recorded at Harold's workshop, 414 Marietta Avenue, Lancaster, PA
Interviewer: Sarah Okafor
Duration: 1 hour 55 minutes

---

Sarah: I want to ask you more about how you actually teach. Like, day to day, what does it look like?

Harold: The first week is observation only. Same as Raymond did with me. I know people think this is cruel — you've come here to learn, you want to do things, and I'm telling you to sit in a chair. But what I've learned is that the first week of observation does two things. First, it teaches you the rhythm of the work — how slowly things move, how much time goes into what looks like a simple task. Second, it tells me something about the apprentice. How they watch. Whether they're seeing the hands or seeing the thought behind the hands.

A few of my apprentices found the first week unbearable. They were always asking questions — not bad questions, just premature questions. "What is that part called?" They want the vocabulary before they have the grammar, if you understand what I mean. The vocabulary will come. The grammar — the underlying sense of what you're trying to accomplish — that has to come first.

Sarah: What's the first thing you actually let them do?

Harold: Clean a movement. Always. I don't care how advanced someone says they are when they walk in — and I've had people come to me after years of self-taught work — the first task is cleaning. Not because cleaning is simple. Because cleaning is when you learn to look.

When you disassemble a movement for cleaning, you have to handle every part. Every wheel, every arbor, every pinion, every spring. You can't clean something you haven't touched. And touching every part forces observation that you'd skip if you were doing a targeted repair. "I'm just here to fix the strike mechanism" means you might miss the worn pivot in the going train that's about to cause a different problem in six months.

I tell every apprentice: the cleaning is the examination. The repair comes after. You can't know what to repair until you've examined everything.

Sarah: What are the most common mistakes beginners make?

Harold: [settling in] I'll give you the full list.

The first and most common: releasing mainspring power without confirming the click is engaged. This is how movements get damaged on the bench. The mainspring holds enormous energy — even a small clock spring can cause significant damage if it releases suddenly. Before you do anything to a movement, you have to know whether power is on the train and deal with it safely. I've had apprentices reach for a wheel and the whole train spins when they move it. That's a near-miss. The next one might not be.

The correct procedure, every time: identify whether the movement is wound or partially wound. Use a let-down key or a finger on the crown wheel to control the power. Release it slowly and in a controlled way before you disassemble. This is not optional. I don't care if you've done it a hundred times — the hundred-and-first time is when you get careless.

Second: over-oiling. New apprentices hear "lubrication is important" and they put oil everywhere. Too much oil is worse than no oil. Excess oil migrates — it travels along pivots by capillary action and gets where you don't want it. It attracts dust. It thickens. The rule is: the right oil, in the right place, in the right quantity. The right quantity is very small. You want to see a meniscus, not a drop. And the right oil matters enormously — clock oil, pallet oil, mainspring oil, they're different viscosities for different purposes.

Third: handling polished surfaces with bare fingers. Fingerprints leave acid that will etch brass over time. Gloves or finger cots, always. I've had apprentices put a beautiful polished brass plate down on the bench, go to lift it, and leave ten fingerprints. That's not a catastrophe but it's unnecessary, and it tells me something about the habits they're forming.

Fourth: forcing things that won't move. If a pivot won't come out, there's a reason. If a wheel won't clear a bridge, there's a reason. The answer is never more force. The answer is more understanding. A pivot that won't come free might have a bent arbor. Or the bridge might be misaligned. Or there might be an unexpected snap ring you haven't noticed. Force is what amateurs use when they don't understand. Understanding is what we use instead.

Fifth — and this one is subtle: reassembling before fully understanding the problem. I see this more in experienced beginners than in true novices. They've cleaned the movement, found something obviously wrong, fixed that thing, and they want to reassemble. But they haven't asked whether there might be more than one problem, or whether the obvious problem was actually a symptom of something else. Take the time. Look at the rest of the movement with the question "what else might be wrong here?" before you close it up.

Sarah: That last one sounds like it comes from experience.

Harold: The 1969 clock. Yes. Every item on that list comes from something that happened, either to me or to an apprentice I was watching. I'm not warning you about theoretical dangers. I'm warning you about things I've seen go wrong.

Sarah: What do you enjoy teaching most?

Harold: Escapement adjustment. Because it's where theory and feel come together most directly.

When you're teaching someone to adjust a verge escapement — the older style, the one you find in 16th and 17th century work and in the earliest American clocks — you can explain the geometry abstractly. The verge sits on its arbor, the pallets engage the escape wheel alternately, the crown wheel drives the pallets. You can draw a diagram and the student understands it intellectually.

But the adjustment is in the feel. The pallets have to engage the crown wheel teeth at the right depth — not too shallow, not too deep. Too shallow and you get "set" — the escapement locks instead of unlocking. Too deep and you get interference, the impulse isn't clean. The adjustment is made by bending the verge — very carefully, very slightly — and then running the escapement by hand and feeling the quality of the impulse.

I can tell a student what "correct" feels like in words. But the moment they feel it themselves, something clicks. I've watched it happen, that moment of recognition. The words become experience.

Sarah: Is there something you wish someone had told you when you were starting?

Harold: That slow is fast. I was an impatient young man — I wanted to be Raymond immediately. I rushed work that should have been slower. I missed things. I had to go back. Every time I rushed and had to go back, the job took longer than if I'd done it slowly the first time.

Now I say this to every apprentice: the job is done when it's done correctly. Not when it's done quickly. A clock that comes back in six months because you rushed a repair is a failure, not a success. The customer doesn't care that it only took you two hours. The clock doesn't care.

Slow is fast. It took me ten years to fully believe it.

Sarah: Let's talk about the future. You mentioned last time that you worry the knowledge will die.

Harold: It will. Parts of it already have. There are clockmakers who were my contemporaries who knew things I don't know — specialized skills, regional traditions, specific techniques for specific types of work. They're gone now, and those things are gone. The books didn't capture them. I didn't know to ask.

There was a man named Georg Böhm who was in his eighties when I was in my forties — Pennsylvania German family, had been in clocks since before World War II. He had a technique for tooth repair on soft metal wheels that I've never seen described anywhere. He showed me once, briefly, and I've approximated it but I've never been sure I do it the way he did. He died in 1994. Whatever exactly he was doing, it's probably gone.

That's one example. Multiply it by every specialist, every regional tradition, every craftsman who worked in isolation and died before anyone thought to record what they knew.

Sarah: Is there any solution?

Harold: Apprenticeships, for as long as possible. Recording. Documentation — not just manuals but the thinking behind the manuals. Why do we do it this way? What happens when you do it wrong? Those things.

I'm not naive about technology. I understand that fewer and fewer young people will sit in a chair and watch an old man work for four months. That time has possibly passed. But the alternative can't be that the knowledge vanishes. If it has to live in a different form than it has before, then it lives in a different form. I'd rather have the knowledge imperfectly transmitted than perfectly lost.

INTERVIEW TRANSCRIPT — HAROLD JENNINGS
Subject: The Hardest Repair — The Dutch Staartklok, 1989; Teaching Philosophy
Recorded at Harold's workshop, 414 Marietta Avenue, Lancaster, PA
Interviewer: Sarah Okafor
Duration: 2 hours 3 minutes

---

Sarah: What's the hardest repair you've ever done?

Harold: [doesn't hesitate] 1989. A Dutch Staartklok — sometimes called a tail clock, or a Frisian clock. The owner was a collector named Marcus van der Berg who had moved here from the Netherlands in the 1960s. He'd brought this clock with him. It had belonged to his grandfather's family in Friesland, probably made around 1820. The movement was brass, which is typical for Dutch work of that period. The case was painted with traditional Frisian motifs — roosters, flowers, a rural scene. Remarkable object.

The presenting problem was that the clock ran for about twenty minutes and then stopped. Mr. van der Berg had taken it to another repairer first — I won't say who — who had serviced it, pronounced it sound, and returned it. It stopped again within the week. Then it came to me.

Sarah: What did you find?

Harold: That's the thing. When I first examined it, I found what the previous repairer had found: a clean movement, properly lubricated, no obvious damage. The train ran freely. The escapement looked right. But the clock stopped.

I ran it under observation for two days on my bench. It stopped four times. I watched it stop once — I was working nearby and heard the tick become uneven and then cease. What I saw when I looked at that moment was the pendulum still swinging but the escapement not unlocking. The impulse wasn't being transmitted.

Now, when the escapement doesn't unlock, you look at the pallet arbor. The arbor carries the pallets and it has to turn freely but with no axial play. If there's axial play — if the arbor can shift along its own axis — the pallets can move out of proper engagement with the escape wheel. Intermittent, temperature-dependent axial play is one of the most difficult problems to diagnose because it doesn't show up consistently.

I tested the arbor play with a gauge and it was within what I'd normally consider acceptable. I tested it again. Still fine. I ran the clock again. It stopped again.

Sarah: How long did this go on?

Harold: Three weeks. I'm not exaggerating. Mr. van der Berg was very patient — he called once a week, I told him we were still investigating, he thanked me and said to take the time I needed. I'm grateful for patients like that. The pressure of a difficult case is already considerable without someone calling daily.

What finally broke it open was a conversation with another member of the NAWCC — a clockmaker from Vermont named Bill Calder who I knew slightly from regional meetings. I described the problem to him at length and he asked me one question: "Have you examined the crutch pin?" 

The crutch is the part that connects the escapement to the pendulum — it has a small pin that engages a slot in the pendulum rod. If the crutch pin is the wrong diameter, or if it's developed a flat spot, it can intermittently fail to transmit the impulse properly.

I examined the crutch pin. Under magnification — and I mean serious magnification, I put it under my watchmaker's loupe at 10x — I could see that it had a tiny, tiny flat spot. Not from wear, but from what looked like an old repair — someone had, at some point in this clock's 170-year history, replaced the crutch pin with a pin that was very slightly too soft. Over time it had developed this flat spot. In most positions of the pendulum swing, it was fine. But at one specific arc of the swing, particularly when the temperature in the shop dropped below 65 degrees — because the pendulum rod changed its stiffness characteristics very slightly — the flat spot caused the pin to momentarily skip the slot instead of engaging it.

Sarah: That's incredibly specific.

Harold: That's the nature of intermittent faults. The most difficult problems in clockmaking aren't the ones where something is obviously broken. Those are easy. The most difficult problems are the ones where something is almost right.

I replaced the crutch pin with correct material. The clock ran for two weeks on my bench without stopping. Then it went home to Mr. van der Berg. He died in 2003 and his son, who I never met, sent me a note to tell me. He mentioned that the clock had run without stopping since 1989.

Sarah: That's a lovely coda to that story.

Harold: It is. That's not always how it ends.

Sarah: Can we shift to teaching? You've had eleven apprentices now.

Harold: Twelve, if you count Michael Ritter who was only here for three months before he decided it wasn't for him. I don't hold that against him. Better to know early.

Sarah: What makes a good apprentice?

Harold: [thinks for a long moment] Patience is the obvious answer and it's not wrong, but it's incomplete. Patience is necessary but not sufficient. I've had patient apprentices who were too passive — they waited to be told everything and never developed instinct.

What I look for is a particular kind of curiosity. Not curiosity about clocks specifically, though that helps. Curiosity about why things work the way they do. The apprentice who holds a movement and wants to understand the logic of it — why did the maker design the striking mechanism this way instead of that way? What problem was he solving? That person will learn.

The ones who don't make it are usually of two types. The first type is impatient in a specific way — they want to be advanced clockmakers immediately, and when the early work is repetitive and slow they become frustrated. Cleaning a movement is not glamorous. You do it a thousand times before it becomes interesting. Some people can't get through the thousand times.

The second type is what I call careful without being thoughtful. They follow the steps correctly — disassembly, cleaning, inspection, reassembly — but they're following steps, not understanding. They can reassemble a movement they've just taken apart, but if I hand them a movement they've never seen and ask them to describe what they think is wrong, they're lost. The steps haven't become understanding.

Sarah: How do you teach someone to develop instinct?

Harold: You can't directly. You can create conditions where it develops. What I do is this: before an apprentice works on any movement, I ask them to examine it and tell me what they think is wrong. Not to diagnose it, necessarily — they may not have the knowledge yet — but to describe what they observe. What does the train feel like when you spin it? Where is the resistance? Is the escapement visibly damaged? What does the case tell you about how this clock was kept?

Then after they've done the work, I ask them what they found versus what they predicted. Over time — and it takes years, not months — they start to see how observations connect to diagnoses. The instinct is the accumulated pattern of those connections.

The other thing I insist on is slowing down. This sounds strange because experienced clockmakers work efficiently. But an apprentice who works at the speed of an experienced clockmaker makes errors. The speed comes after the care. If I see someone rushing, I ask them to stop and explain what they're doing. Not as a punishment — just as a discipline. You can't explain what you're doing if you don't know what you're doing. The explanation enforces the understanding.

Sarah: What do you tell an apprentice when they make a mistake?

Harold: I ask them what they were thinking when they made the decision that led to the mistake. Not accusatorially — genuinely. Because usually the decision was reasonable given what they knew, and the lesson isn't "don't make that decision" but "here's what you needed to know to make a better one."

Raymond used to say the clock doesn't know you meant well. I use that too. But I pair it with something I believe equally: the mistake you understand is worth more than the success you can't explain.

If an apprentice breaks a click spring, that's frustrating. But if they understand why — they released the power without checking whether the click was engaged, or they applied force in the wrong direction — that's a lesson that will stay with them. The broken click spring is the tuition. If they break it and don't understand why, they've paid the tuition and learned nothing.

Sarah: You've mentioned a few times that you're worried about the craft dying. Is that real, or is it something older generations always say?

Harold: [long pause] It's real. I know it's the kind of thing older people say. My father said the watchmaker was dying when I was young. He wasn't entirely wrong — quartz movement did change watchmaking significantly. But mechanical clockmaking is different, I think.

Here's the specific worry: the knowledge is in people, not in books. There are good books — Britten's, Rawlings, the Goodrich book on American clocks. But the hands-on knowledge, the feel of a correctly running train, the sound of an escapement that's about to fail, how much pressure to use on a pivot that won't come free — that's not in books. That's in people. And those people are old.

I'm eighty-one. I still work every day that my hands cooperate. But I'm aware that the number of days is finite in a way that I wasn't aware of at fifty. There are things I know — things I learned from Raymond, who learned them from the German, who learned them from God knows who in Europe — that exist nowhere except in my hands and my head. When I die, those things die.

That's why I take apprentices seriously. And why I'm willing to talk to you for hours, even when I could be at the bench.

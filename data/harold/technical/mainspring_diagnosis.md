HAROLD JENNINGS — TECHNICAL NOTES
Subject: Mainspring Diagnosis, Failure Modes, Replacement
Written for apprentice reference, revised 2018
414 Marietta Avenue, Lancaster, PA

---

The mainspring is the engine of the clock. It's also, in my experience, the most misunderstood part. Apprentices tend to focus on the going train and the escapement — which are more interesting, I grant you — and treat the mainspring as background. That's wrong. A mainspring problem will defeat a perfect escapement every time.

---

WHAT A MAINSPRING DOES

A mainspring is a long, thin ribbon of hardened steel coiled inside a cylindrical barrel. One end (the outer end) is attached to the barrel wall. The other end (the inner end) is attached to the arbor that runs through the center of the barrel. When you wind the clock, you rotate the arbor, coiling the spring tighter around it. The spring's tension to uncoil is what drives the gear train.

A good mainspring delivers relatively even power throughout its run — from fully wound to nearly run down. In practice, no spring delivers perfectly even power; all springs are stronger when fully wound and weaker when nearly run down. The design of the going train takes this into account, but excessive variation (from a spring that's the wrong size, deteriorated, or failing) will show up as inconsistent timekeeping — gaining when freshly wound, losing as it runs down.

---

MAINSPRING FAILURE MODES

I've seen mainsprings fail in every way a spring can fail. Here are the most common:

1. Fatigue fracture. A mainspring subjected to too many winding cycles, or that was wound too tightly at some point, develops metal fatigue. Fatigue cracks begin at stress concentrations — usually the inner coils near the arbor hook, or in any location where there's a small nick or surface defect. The spring runs fine until the crack propagates far enough, and then it breaks. Often without warning.

When a mainspring breaks under tension, the sudden power release can do significant damage to the gear train if the click mechanism is functioning. If the click is worn or not engaging properly, the energy has to go somewhere and it goes through the train forward, very fast. Wheels can be damaged, arbors bent.

Prevention: inspect the inner coils carefully during every service. Use magnification. Look for discoloration (usually a darker area around a developing crack), surface pitting, or any crack beginning to develop. A spring that shows these signs should be replaced preventively.

2. Set (permanent deformation). Over time, a mainspring that is kept wound continuously — as most running clocks are — may take a "set": the steel deforms plastically and the spring no longer returns to its fully relaxed curve. A spring with severe set doesn't deliver full power even when wound. The clock loses power earlier in its run cycle.

You detect set by carefully removing the spring from the barrel and observing its natural curve. A spring in good condition coils in a consistent, fair curve. A spring with significant set has flat areas or kinks — places where the curvature changes abruptly. These are the areas where the metal has permanently deformed.

Mild set may be acceptable; severe set means replacement.

3. Rust and corrosion. A spring in a clock that was kept in a damp environment may be rusted. Surface rust weakens the spring and concentrates stress at corroded spots. Light surface oxidation can sometimes be cleaned; actual pitting means the spring is compromised and should be replaced.

I had a Vienna Regulator in 2001 — a beautiful piece, early 19th century, had been in a carriage house that wasn't well sealed. The spring had surface rust along most of its length. I cleaned what I could, but there were several pitted areas on the inner coils that made me uncomfortable. I recommended replacement to the owner. He asked if it might be fine. I told him it might be fine for years, or it might fail tomorrow, and I couldn't tell him which. He chose replacement. Good decision.

4. Wrong spring fitted. Someone, at some point in the clock's history, fitted a replacement spring of incorrect dimensions. This happens more than you'd think — springs were and are sold in nominal sizes that don't always match the actual barrel requirements. A spring that's too thick for the barrel will bind; one that's too thin won't deliver enough power; one that's too long may not fully engage the arbor hook.

When I service a clock and the power delivery seems inconsistent in a way I can't otherwise explain, I measure the spring against the barrel. The standard rules for spring sizing: the spring in its relaxed state should fill roughly two-thirds of the barrel cross section. The spring width should be slightly less than the barrel depth. The spring length and thickness should be specified by the movement design; Britten's or the movement manufacturer's specs are your reference if you can find them.

---

EXAMINING THE BARREL AND ARBOR

Don't just examine the spring. The barrel and arbor must also be inspected.

The barrel wall should be smooth inside. Any roughness or score marks indicate the spring has been rubbing against the wall, which causes wear on the spring's outer surface and heat from friction. Clean carefully and determine why the rubbing is occurring — it may mean the spring is too thick or too long, or the barrel cover isn't seating properly.

The arbor hook (the small projection on the arbor that holds the inner end of the spring) must be intact and correctly shaped. A damaged or worn arbor hook can cause the spring to slip, resulting in a clock that loses power gradually even when freshly wound. The click end of the arbor should show no wear on the ratchet wheel teeth.

The barrel cover must seat properly. A cover that's warped or not seating flush can allow the spring to ride up on one side during operation, causing binding and inconsistent power delivery. I've seen covers that were replaced at some point with incorrect replacements that don't quite fit — the clock runs but runs badly.

---

LETTING DOWN POWER BEFORE DISASSEMBLY

I've put this in the escapement notes too, but it bears repeating because it's how movements get damaged on the bench.

Before you disassemble any movement, establish whether power is on the train. If the clock is wound or partially wound, you must let down the power before removing any parts.

The correct method: hold the clickwork with a finger or a let-down key. Release the click. Allow the spring to uncoil slowly, controlling it through the click mechanism. This takes time. Do not rush it. A fully wound clock may take several minutes to let down completely.

The wrong method: remove parts while power is on the train. I've seen this go wrong in multiple ways. I had an apprentice — 1997, good student otherwise — who was working on an eight-day mantel clock and removed the click spring before letting down the power. The train spun. The hands ran. Nothing broke that time. He was lucky. I told him he would not be lucky the next time, and that there might not be a next time to learn from.

The click is your safety mechanism. Respect it.

---

MAINSPRING OIL

Use mainspring oil (sometimes called barrel lubricant or clock grease). It's a grease formulation, not a thin oil — it stays in the barrel and doesn't migrate.

Application: clean the barrel and spring thoroughly first. Then apply a thin, even coat of mainspring grease to both surfaces of the spring. Not dripping — a thin, even coat you can almost not see.

The purpose: to reduce friction between coils as the spring coils and uncoils, which both extends spring life and makes power delivery more even. An unlubricated spring wears faster and delivers less even power.

Do not use clock pallet oil in the barrel. It's too thin — it will be flung out of the barrel by centrifugal force as the barrel rotates and will end up where you don't want it.

---

WHEN TO REPLACE VERSUS SERVICE

A question I get regularly: when should a mainspring be replaced versus cleaned and serviced?

My criteria for replacement:
- Any visible crack, even small
- Corrosion pitting in more than two or three small spots
- Significant permanent set (flat areas, kinks)
- Width markedly less than barrel depth (indicates someone has replaced it with a narrower spring before, or it's stretched)
- Outer end clip damaged or missing
- Inner end hook hole cracked or deformed

My criteria for service and retain:
- Light surface oxidation, no pitting
- Spring dimensions match barrel correctly
- Uniform curve when removed from barrel (no significant set)
- No cracks or visible fatigue indicators

When in doubt: replace. A mainspring costs a few dollars. A damaged gear train from a broken spring costs far more in time and sometimes in irreplaceable parts.

I've made the mistake of keeping a spring I had doubts about twice in my career. Both times it failed within a year. I don't keep doubtful springs anymore.
